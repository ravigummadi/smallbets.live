# Student Micro-Funding Program - Product Plan

## Context
Conversation between Gummadi and Praveen exploring a student funding model. Core insight: AI/tooling has reduced the cost to build software products from ~$100K to ~$10K, creating an opportunity to fund college students directly.

---

## The Thesis

**Core Bet**: First and second-year college students in India can build meaningful products if given $10K funding + small incentives (summer stipend, iPhone).

**Why Now**:
- AI coding assistants (Claude, Copilot) have 10x'd developer productivity
- Cloud costs at near-zero for MVPs
- No-code/low-code tools democratized shipping

**Target**: 1st and 2nd year students (not 3rd/4th who are focused on internships/placements)

**Go-to-Market**: Direct to students, bypassing professors

---

## Proposed Model

| Component | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Funding Amount | ~$10K per student/team |
| Additional Incentives | Summer stipend, iPhone |
| Target Cohort | 1st-2nd year engineering students in India |
| Selection | Direct outreach (not professor referrals) |

---

## Critical Debate

### Arguments FOR This Model

1. **Cost arbitrage is real** - Indian students have lower living costs, $10K goes further
2. **Timing advantage** - 1st/2nd years aren't distracted by placement season
3. **Hunger factor** - These students have something to prove, unlike comfortable 4th years
4. **AI leverage** - A motivated freshman with Claude can now ship what took teams of 5 before
5. **Direct relationship** - Bypassing professors means no gatekeepers, pure signal

### Arguments AGAINST This Model (Fierce Debate)

1. **Selection Problem is Brutal**
   - How do you find exceptional 1st years who haven't proven anything yet?
   - No track record, no GitHub history, no shipped products
   - You're essentially betting on raw potential with zero signal
   - *Counter*: "The professor route" solves this but you've rejected it

2. **Maturity Gap**
   - 18-19 year olds have never managed money, time, or projects independently
   - College 1st years are still figuring out how to do laundry
   - $10K to someone who's never had $1K is a recipe for waste
   - *Counter*: That's the whole point - you're buying optionality cheap

3. **The "Janjoo Professor" Argument is Weak**
   - Dismissing ALL professors as incompetent is intellectually lazy
   - IIT/IIIT faculty have deep industry connections and can spot talent
   - You're letting ego ("we know better") override a functional filter
   - *Counter*: Professors optimize for academic success, not startup success

4. **Incentive Misalignment**
   - An iPhone is a consumption good, not a tool that forces output
   - Summer stipend without milestones = vacation money
   - Where are the forcing functions? Equity stakes? Demo days?
   - *Counter*: Low-commitment funding attracts more diverse applicants

5. **Why Would They Pick You?**
   - $10K is meaningful but not life-changing
   - What's the differentiation vs. college entrepreneurship cells, ACM chapters, or existing grants?
   - No mentor network mentioned, no community, no brand
   - *Counter*: Most existing programs are bureaucratic nightmares

6. **Scale Economics Don't Work**
   - To find one outlier, you need 100 bets
   - 100 × $10K = $1M deployed for maybe 1-2 hits
   - That's worse than angel investing hit rates
   - *Counter*: You're not looking for unicorns, you're building a talent pipeline

7. **The Real Competition**
   - GSoC (Google Summer of Code) offers stipend + prestige + mentorship
   - MLH Fellowship, Buildspace, On Deck
   - These have brand, community, and alumni networks
   - *Counter*: Those are competitive and narrow; this could be higher volume

---

## What's Missing From This Plan

1. **Selection criteria** - How do you actually identify who to fund?
2. **Structure** - Equity? Grants? Loans? Revenue share?
3. **Support model** - Just money or mentorship too?
4. **Success metrics** - What does "winning" look like?
5. **Deal flow** - How do students find out about this?
6. **Exit thesis** - Are you building a fund? An acqui-hire pipeline? A community?

---

## Strongest Counter-Position

**The professor route might actually be correct.**

Not because professors are great at spotting startup talent, but because:
- They have repeated access to cohorts (you see 500 students/year)
- Commission incentivizes them to recommend genuinely good candidates
- They filter out the obvious failures for free
- You can always override their recommendations

The "janjoo professor" objection assumes all professors are equally bad. Reality: 5% are exceptional talent scouts. Find those 5% and give them incentives.

---

## Verdict

**The thesis has legs, but the execution plan is underbaked.**

Core insight (AI reduced build costs → fund younger students cheaper) is valid and worth pursuing. But the plan as stated is more vibes than strategy:

- No selection mechanism
- No structure
- No differentiation
- Emotional rejection of professor channel without alternative

**Recommendation**: Before deploying capital, run a pilot:
1. Fund 5 students via direct outreach
2. Fund 5 students via professor referral
3. Compare outcomes after 6 months
4. Let data kill the debate

## REVISED PLAN: Talent Pipeline Model

### Goal Clarification
This is NOT a fund seeking returns. This is a **talent identification and development pipeline** to find exceptional builders before they're obvious.

### Why This Changes Everything

| Fund Model | Talent Pipeline Model |
|------------|----------------------|
| Need equity/returns | No equity needed |
| Success = company exits | Success = exceptional hires |
| High selection bar | Can afford more bets |
| Distant relationship | Close mentorship |
| One-time interaction | Multi-year relationship |

### Revised Economics

**Traditional hiring cost**: $30-50K for a senior developer in India (agency fees, interviews, onboarding risk)

**Pipeline cost**: $10K × 5 students = $50K → if ONE becomes a great hire, you've broken even. If you get access to their network, you're ahead.

**Real arbitrage**: You're paying to see people perform under real conditions before anyone else does.

---

## Concrete Talent Pipeline Design

### Selection (Solving the Hard Problem)

**Don't guess. Make them prove it.**

1. **24-hour Build Challenge**
   - Give a prompt: "Build [X] that solves [Y]"
   - Judge on: shipping speed, code quality, product taste
   - This is YOUR interview process, compressed

2. **Public Building Requirement**
   - They must build in public (Twitter/YouTube)
   - You see work ethic, communication, and consistency
   - Natural filter: performative people quit after 2 weeks

3. **Referral Bonus Structure**
   - Every funded student gets $500 if they refer someone who gets funded
   - Creates viral loop within college networks
   - Students know who the real builders are better than you do

### Structure

**No equity. Strings attached differently.**

- $10K grant, disbursed in 3 tranches ($3K/$4K/$3K)
- Monthly 30-min check-ins
- Public demo every 6 weeks
- **Right of first conversation** for any job/internship for 2 years
- Access to private Slack/Discord with other funded students

### Why No Professors (Validated)

For a **talent pipeline**, professor referrals are actually wrong:

- Professors optimize for "good student" not "good builder"
- Academic success ≠ shipping ability
- You want misfits who hack on side projects, not grade-chasers
- Direct outreach finds the kids professors overlook

### Forcing Functions

| Milestone | Trigger | Consequence |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| Week 4 | First public demo | If nothing shipped, pause funding |
| Week 8 | User feedback collected | Must show real usage |
| Week 12 | Case study written | Document what was learned |
| Week 16 | Final demo + retro | Funding complete |

### Deal Flow Channels

1. **Hackathon sponsorship** - $2K to sponsor, access to top 10 teams
2. **Twitter/X presence** - "We fund college builders, not companies"
3. **Word of mouth** - Funded students become recruiters
4. **GitHub scouting** - Find active contributors in popular repos

## 90-Day Pilot Plan

**Week 1-2**: Design
- Build landing page
- Create application form
- Design 24-hour build challenge

**Week 3-4**: Recruit
- Post on Twitter, college subreddits, WhatsApp groups
- Target: 50 applications

**Week 5-6**: Select
- Run build challenge
- Pick 5 students

**Week 7-12**: Execute
- Disburse first tranche
- Weekly check-ins
- First public demo at Week 8

**Week 12**: Evaluate
- Who shipped?
- Who would you hire?
- What broke?
